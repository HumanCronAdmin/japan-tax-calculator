Japan Tax FAQ for Foreigners: 7 Burning Questions Answered

Updated April 14, 2026

Do freelancers in Japan pay taxes differently?

The short answer is yes—but only in how the paperwork looks. Freelancers report income using Form A (確定申告書A) instead of the salaried employee form (確定申告書B), and you claim business expenses separately. The tax rates themselves don’t change. I’ve seen friends start freelancing and panic when their first tax bill arrived, only to realize the numbers weren’t actually different from their salary days—just organized differently.

Something that trips up new freelancers is mixing personal and business expenses. That 10,000-yen lunch with a client? Deductible. The same meal eaten alone on a bad day? Not deductible. One friend tried claiming his entire train pass, then spent an afternoon on the phone with a tax office volunteer explaining why commuting to his home office didn’t count. Pro tip: Get a separate bank account for business transactions. It makes tracking expenses easier, and the tax office loves clean records.

What’s this ‘residency tax’ everyone keeps mentioning?

Residency tax (住民税) is what your local ward office sends you around June each year. It’s based on last year’s income but calculated separately from your national income tax. The key thing foreigners miss is that even if you leave Japan mid-year, you’ll still owe residency tax for that year unless you file a special form (離脱届) before leaving.

A coworker once told me about her panic when she received a 150,000-yen bill for residency tax on a salary of 3 million yen the previous year. Turns out, she’d forgotten that her company handled only national taxes through payroll. Local taxes were her responsibility all along. If you’re on a work visa, check whether your company includes residency tax in your salary package. Some do, many don’t.

Can I deduct my rent if I’m a tenant?

No. Unlike some countries, Japan doesn’t allow rent deductions for personal residences. I still remember the first time a client asked me this—I had to explain that Japanese tax law views rent as a living expense, not a business one. The exception? If you run a home office and deduct a portion of your apartment as a business expense, but that comes with strict rules about square footage and usage.

Where you can save is on health insurance premiums and pension contributions. Those are fully deductible. One friend saved over 200,000 yen last year just by remembering to include his national health insurance payments in his deductions. Always keep those premium notices—they’re gold for tax season.

Do I need to file taxes if my income is low?

It depends on your visa type and total income. For most work visas, if your annual income is below 2 million yen, you’re technically exempt from filing—but only if your employer already withheld taxes. The moment you freelance or have side income, you’re back in the filing zone. I’ve lost count of how many people I’ve met who assumed their part-time income was too small to matter, only to get a nasty surprise when their residency tax caught up with them.

The 2-million-yen threshold is a common trap. It’s based on gross income, not profit. So if you earned 1.8 million yen at a part-time job but had 200,000 yen in deductible expenses, you technically owe nothing—but you still need to file to prove it. Keep all your receipts regardless.

How do I handle taxes if I’m paid in foreign currency?

Convert everything to yen using the Bank of Japan’s exchange rate on the day you received payment. Don’t use your personal bank’s rate or the day you converted the money—that’s a mistake I see often. Tools like Wise or Revolut show the daily BoJ rate if you need it, though their rates include small fees.

A freelancer friend once tried to use his bank’s exchange rate from the day he transferred money to his account. The tax office rejected it because the rate was three days old. He had to recalculate everything, which cost him hours of work. Pro tip: Screenshot the BoJ rate on the day you earn the money and save it with your records. No arguing with the tax office later.

What happens if I miss the filing deadline?

Penalties start at 5% of unpaid tax for the first month, then 1% per month after that. If you owe 500,000 yen and file two months late, you’re looking at an extra 10,000 yen in penalties. Not catastrophic, but annoying. The real trouble comes if you owe tax and don’t file at all—the penalty jumps to 15% of the unpaid amount after six months.

I’ve seen people panic because they thought the deadline was March 15 like US taxes. In Japan, it’s usually March 15 for paper filings, but online filings give you an extra month until April 15. If you’re filing late, do it as soon as possible. The tax office is more forgiving if you show initiative.

One friend filed three weeks late and got a polite letter asking for an explanation. He wrote back explaining he’d just moved, and they waived the penalty. Had he ignored it, the penalty would’ve stuck.

Is there an English-speaking tax accountant I can trust?

Yes, but your options depend on where you live. In Tokyo, firms like Tax Lab Japan or Tokyo Tax Consulting have English-speaking staff. In Osaka, KPMG Japan offers English support. For smaller cities, check with your local ward office—they often have lists of bilingual volunteers who help foreigners for free.

A common mistake is assuming any accountant who speaks English knows Japanese tax law. Tax Lab Japan specializes in foreign residents, while a generalist firm might miss niche deductions. Always ask: “Have you filed taxes for foreigners in Japan before?” If they hesitate, keep looking.

What’s the one thing everyone gets wrong about Japanese taxes?

Assuming their home country’s tax treaty covers everything. Japan has treaties with over 70 countries, but they don’t eliminate all double taxation. For example, the US-Japan treaty prevents double taxation on income, but you still need to file in both countries. I’ve met Americans who skipped their US taxes thinking the treaty protected them, only to owe back taxes plus penalties years later.

The second mistake? Confusing residency tax with income tax. They’re calculated separately and sent at different times. Residency tax comes in June based on last year’s income, while income tax is filed in March. Mix them up, and you’ll think your tax bill is higher than it actually is.

Pro tip: Set up automatic reminders for June (residency tax bill) and March (income tax filing). A simple calendar alert saves more headaches than you’d think.
